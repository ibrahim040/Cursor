# Contributing Factors for Intrusion and Non-intrusion Work Zone Crashes: A Comparative Review

## 1. INTRODUCTION

The construction, rehabilitation, and routine maintenance of highway infrastructure require temporary work zones that change traffic patterns and create a dynamic operating environment for both road users and workers on foot. While these zones are essential for sustaining the transportation network, they are consistently linked to a measurable rise in crash risk relative to comparable roadway sections without active construction. In 2023, 899 individuals were killed in work zone related crashes in the United States, a figure that reflects the persistence of this safety problem despite many years of research, engineering refinement, and regulatory change (U.S. Department of Transportation, 2025). The same source reports that, in 2022, speed was a contributing factor in 34% of fatal work zone events, rear end collisions accounted for 21% of those fatalities, and commercial motor vehicles were involved in roughly 30% of fatal work zone crashes. Between 2020 and 2021, work zone fatalities rose by 10.8% nationally, followed by a decline of nearly 7% between 2021 and 2023, which suggests that targeted interventions can moderate, but not eliminate, the toll (U.S. Department of Transportation, 2025).

Work zone crashes are not a single homogeneous phenomenon, and a useful but underexplored distinction separates intrusion events from non-intrusion events. An intrusion (sometimes called an encroachment in the literature) occurs when an errant vehicle leaves the active travel lane and enters the designated workspace, bringing moving traffic into the area where construction personnel, equipment, and materials are located (Bryden et al., 2000; Ullman et al., 2011). A non-intrusion crash, in contrast, takes place within the active travel lanes and does not breach the workspace itself; the dominant subtype of this category is the rear end collision (Yang et al., 2015). The distinction matters because intrusion events place workers directly in the path of moving vehicles, transforming an event that might otherwise produce only property damage into a potentially fatal occupational exposure. Recent national summaries indicate that, between 2021 and 2023, more than half of road worker fatalities resulted from workers on foot being struck by vehicles inside the active work zone (National Work Zone Safety Information Clearinghouse, 2025). Although non-intrusion crashes do not threaten workers in the same direct manner and tend to be far more frequent overall (Yang et al., 2015), it is the intrusion category that drives the highest worker fatality risk and therefore deserves particular attention in research and countermeasure design.

The body of work on work zone safety has grown considerably over the last two decades, but most of this literature has examined non-intrusion events, particularly rear end collisions, queue related crashes, and overall work zone crash frequency and severity (Yang et al., 2015; Khattak et al., 2002; Ghasemzadeh and Ahmed, 2019; Mokhtarimousavi et al., 2019; Nasrollahzadeh et al., 2021). The volume of work focused specifically on intrusion or encroachment events is comparatively small (Bryden et al., 2000; Ullman et al., 2010; Ullman et al., 2011; Thapa and Mishra, 2021; Das et al., 2023), and a side by side empirical comparison of contributing factors for intrusion and non-intrusion crashes within a single dataset and modeling framework is not evident in the studies reviewed here. The papers that do address one of the two categories are typically built on data from a single state. Examples include the Bryden et al. (2000) review of intrusion incidents on New York State Department of Transportation projects, the Das et al. (2023) cluster correspondence analysis of Texas DOT data from 2016 through 2019, the Khattak et al. (2002) negative binomial analysis of 36 freeway projects in California, the Ghasemzadeh and Ahmed (2019) ordered probit study of Washington State data from 2009 through 2013, the Li and Bai (2009) logistic regression analysis of Kansas crashes, the Mokhtarimousavi et al. (2019) support vector machine analysis of Florida data, and the Nasrollahzadeh et al. (2021) machine learning analysis of California roadside work zones from 2013 through 2018. Some shared behavioral and environmental conditions appear repeatedly across both categories, including driver inattention and distraction, speed non compliance, lighting deficits, lane closures, and the presence of large trucks (Yang et al., 2015; Dingus et al., 2016; Zhang and Hassan, 2019; Nasrollahzadeh et al., 2021). Other factors are more closely tied to one category than the other; delineation quality, intrusion alert system performance, and physical barrier deployment are most directly relevant to intrusion events (Bryden et al., 2000; Thapa and Mishra, 2021), while queue warning effectiveness and the speed differential between approach speeds and reduced work zone limits are most directly relevant to rear end non-intrusion events (Yang et al., 2015; U.S. Department of Transportation, 2025). Understanding which factors operate in common, which operate distinctly, and which carry the greatest weight for intrusion crashes specifically is important because controlling those factors offers the most direct route to reducing worker fatalities.

This paper addresses these gaps by synthesizing the literature on contributing factors for intrusion related and non-intrusion related work zone crashes, identifying factors that are common to both categories and those that distinguish them, and outlining the methodological positions and limitations of the existing studies as a foundation for a unified empirical analysis.

## 2. LITERATURE REVIEW

### 2.1 Overview of Work Zone Safety Research

Work zones present a distinct traffic environment in which temporary geometric changes, lower speed limits, lane shifts, narrowed lanes, and the proximity of workers and heavy equipment to active traffic combine to elevate crash risk. Yang et al. (2015) carried out a state-of-the-art review of work zone safety literature and confirmed that the presence of a work zone is generally associated with elevated crash rates relative to comparable non work zone segments, with rear end collisions emerging as the most consistently reported crash type. The same review documented inconsistencies in the reported direction and magnitude of effects for variables such as weather, time of day, traffic control device configuration, and facility type, which the authors attributed to differences in methods, data limitations, and the heterogeneity of work zone configurations across states.

The scale of the problem is substantial. The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (2023b) reported that more than 102,000 work zone crashes occurred in the United States in 2020, equivalent to a crash roughly every 5.2 minutes. Sustained attention from FHWA and state agencies has produced a wide range of countermeasures, including temporary rumble strips, variable speed limit systems, intrusion alert technology, and additional enforcement.

Methodological practice in this field has evolved over time. Early studies relied primarily on descriptive analysis of police crash records, while more recent work has used logistic regression, ordered probit and ordered logit models, negative binomial and Poisson count models, mixed logit and random parameters frameworks that allow for unobserved heterogeneity, and machine learning techniques such as random forests, support vector machines, and gradient boosting (Khattak et al., 2002; Li and Bai, 2009; Ghasemzadeh and Ahmed, 2019; Mokhtarimousavi et al., 2019; Zhang and Hassan, 2019; Nasrollahzadeh et al., 2021; Islam, 2022). Each generation of methods has improved the ability to isolate effects of specific contributing factors, but inconsistent and incomplete coding of work zone crash data across states continues to constrain generalizability (Yang et al., 2015; Nasrollahzadeh et al., 2021).

### 2.2 Non-intrusion Related Work Zone Crashes

Non-intrusion work zone crashes occur within the active travel lanes, with the errant vehicle remaining inside the lane and not crossing into the designated workspace. The most frequent subtype is the rear end collision, which arises when motorists fail to slow enough to match the reduced speeds of traffic queued ahead of, or within, the work zone. Rear end collisions remain among the most frequently identified fatal work zone crash types in national summaries and are the most consistently identified crash type across the broader literature (U.S. Department of Transportation, 2025; Yang et al., 2015). Sideswipe and angle crashes form secondary non-intrusion subtypes that arise from constrained lane widths, unexpected merging requirements, and reduced lateral clearances along active work zone corridors.

Speed related behavior is the central driver of rear end crash risk in work zones. The transition from prevailing highway speeds to lower work zone limits creates a speed differential that, combined with insufficient following distance or driver inattention, produces rear end collisions. Speed was reported as a contributing factor in 34% of all fatal work zone crashes in 2022 (U.S. Department of Transportation, 2025), and roughly 32% of work zone fatalities in 2021 were linked to speeding (U.S. Department of Transportation, FHWA, n.d.). The severity of the speed differential is amplified on high volume facilities where queues form quickly, shrinking the window available to drivers to detect and react to decelerating vehicles ahead.

Empirical studies of non-intrusion crashes have spanned several states and methodological traditions. Khattak et al. (2002) used the Highway Safety Information System database to assemble crash records from 36 California freeway projects and applied negative binomial models to compare property damage only and injury crashes before and during work zone activity. Their analysis covered 8,090 reported crashes and reported increases of 23.8% in property damage only crashes, 17.4% in injury crashes, and 21.5% in total crashes during work zone periods relative to before. Nasrollahzadeh et al. (2021) developed a roadside work zone collision dataset for California covering the years 2013 through 2018, used 42 features describing time, location, work zone characteristics, traffic volume, and road properties, and applied an extreme gradient boosting classifier alongside statistical analysis. They identified four leading predictors of work zone collision risk: lane closure status, work zone length, historical collision density in the surrounding area, and the truck share of traffic volume. Lane closures elevated risk by compressing roadway width, requiring merging maneuvers, and creating queuing conditions, while longer work zones increased cumulative exposure along the corridor, and higher truck volume amplified risk through longer stopping distances and greater impact severity.

Traffic control device adequacy and the effectiveness of advance warning are central to the prevention of non-intrusion crashes. Work zones with poorly timed advance warning sign sequences, weak queue warning, or inadequate speed transition measures generate higher approach speeds that translate into a higher probability of rear end collisions. Khattak et al. (2002) found that the presence of a work zone systematically shifted the ratio of injury to non injury crashes relative to comparable non work zone locations, which suggests that the work zone environment intensifies crash consequences even when the precipitating factors, such as following too closely or speed limit non compliance, are not unique to work zones.

Environmental and weather conditions also play a sizeable role. Ghasemzadeh and Ahmed (2019) used an ordered probit model with five years of Washington State work zone crash data and reported that weather and lighting conditions ranked among the most important determinants of injury severity, with crashes during dusk and dawn periods more likely to result in severe outcomes. Reduced visibility from precipitation, glare, or transitional lighting hampers the ability of drivers to detect queue formation at distances sufficient for safe stopping, which in turn elevates rear end collision probability and severity.

The participation of large commercial vehicles introduces additional severity pathways. Islam (2022) applied random parameters logit models with heterogeneity in means and variances to driver injury severity in single vehicle large truck crashes in Florida from 2011 through 2019, comparing work zone with non work zone outcomes. The estimates indicated that, in work zones, the likelihood of severe driver injury was about 14 times higher on rural and 6 times higher on urban interstate highways for large truck drivers compared with non work zones, and about 1.3 times lower when large trucks were operating 10 mph below the posted speed limit. Heavy trucks above 26,000 pounds, lane shift work zone configurations, and careless driving were all linked with higher injury severity. The combination of geometric lane restrictions, mandatory speed reductions, and the dynamic queuing environment of work zones creates conditions under which large truck crashes produce more severe injury outcomes than crashes on unrestricted segments.

Despite this body of work, several gaps remain. Most non-intrusion analyses are restricted to a single state, focus on aggregate work zone crashes rather than the specific mechanism that initiates the event, and do not separate crashes that breach the workspace from those that remain in the travel lane. Consequently, the existing non-intrusion literature offers limited insight into how the contributing factors it identifies might operate differently when the crash mechanism shifts from in lane speed management failure to path deviation into the workspace.

### 2.3 Intrusion Related Work Zone Crashes

A work zone intrusion occurs when a vehicle leaves its intended travel path and enters the designated workspace, placing moving traffic in direct proximity to construction workers, equipment, and materials. Ullman et al. (2011) categorized intrusion related crashes into two behavioral pathways. Inadvertent intrusions are typically associated with driver inattention, late perception of work zone controls, or inability to respond to lane restrictions in time. Intentional intrusions occur when drivers deliberately enter closed or restricted areas to access exit ramps, driveways, or intersections, or choose to ignore flaggers. The distinction is meaningful because the underlying causes differ, and countermeasures must therefore be tailored either to perceptual failures or to deliberate noncompliance.

Although intrusion crashes account for a small share of total work zone crashes, their consequences are often severe. Ullman et al. (2010) reported that intrusion crashes made up 7.5% of work zone crashes during daytime operations and 12.4% during nighttime operations. They also found that such events were more often associated with collisions involving construction equipment, work vehicles, or materials and debris than with personnel, with about 17% resulting in direct worker impact. Even so, the severity implications are substantial, since workers near active traffic are highly exposed when these events occur. In 2023, 176 pedestrians were killed in work zone crashes, a category that may include workers on foot but is not limited to them (National Work Zone Safety Information Clearinghouse, 2025).

Driver inattention and distraction emerge consistently as leading precursors to inadvertent intrusion. Das et al. (2023) applied cluster correspondence analysis to four years of Texas Department of Transportation crash data covering 2016 through 2019 and identified driver inattention as a primary encroachment factor, often compounded by abrupt changes in traffic flow that prevented drivers from making timely path corrections. Their analysis identified three dominant clusters in the fatal and injury data, namely median related crashes on two lane divided high volume roadways, single vehicle rollover events on two way divided roadways with unprotected medians, and overturning crashes on two lane undivided roadways operating under controlled traffic. These clusters indicate that certain roadway configurations, especially those involving medians and divided arrangements, systematically concentrate encroachment risk.

Speed is closely tied to intrusion causation. When approach speeds are inconsistent with the path adjustments required by the work zone, drivers face a higher probability of failing to complete the necessary lane transitions within the available distance. Ullman et al. (2008) showed that intrusion crash risk is elevated under dark conditions, attributable to the reduced conspicuity of channelizing devices, pavement markings, and advance warning signs at night. Zhang and Hassan (2019), using mixed multinomial logit models on long term highway work zone data, found that exceeding posted speed limits was among the strongest predictors of elevated injury severity in nighttime work zone crashes.

Roadway geometry compounds intrusion risk. Curved alignments shorten available sight distance and reduce the time window for path correction. Das et al. (2023) identified curved roadway segments as a significant factor associated with encroachment crashes, consistent with the higher lateral path control demand at moments when attention is already drawn to the modified work zone environment. Ghasemzadeh and Ahmed (2019) likewise reported that the complexity of the driving task at intersection influence areas intensified the severity of adverse weather work zone crashes.

Work zone configuration and the performance of intrusion alert technology also affect the outcome of an intrusion event. Thapa and Mishra (2021) used worker reactions during emulated intrusion experiments and survival analysis to evaluate three Work Zone Intrusion Alert Systems and identified the speed of the intruding vehicle, the distance between the sensor and the worker, and the accuracy of the system in detecting intrusions and alerting workers as the key determinants of crash occurrence in the presence of these systems. Their findings argued for standardized setup and matched use cases for the different systems.

A small set of related work also addresses the night versus day dimension that is relevant to intrusion events without focusing exclusively on intrusion mechanisms. Ullman et al. (2008) provided a traffic safety evaluation of nighttime and daytime work zones that supported later intrusion specific analyses, and Zhang and Hassan (2019) examined long term highway work zone crashes by daylight condition and reported that several variables exhibit different directions or magnitudes of effect across the two conditions.

Even with these contributions, several limitations stand out. Existing intrusion specific studies are typically built on data from a single state, often relying on descriptive or cluster based analysis rather than predictive or severity oriented modeling, and they have not been integrated with the much larger non-intrusion literature in a way that would isolate the marginal contribution of intrusion specific factors. The next subsection draws together the factors that the literature treats as common to both crash categories before turning to those that are mechanistically distinct.

### 2.4 Common Contributing Factors Across Both Crash Types

A consistent set of underlying conditions appears across both crash categories. Driver inattention and distraction, speed non compliance, and reduced cognitive engagement with the driving task are the most pervasive shared contributors, whether they end in a path deviation into the workspace or in a failure to brake ahead of a queued vehicle (Mokhtarimousavi et al., 2019; Das et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2015). Human factors are estimated to contribute to roughly 87.7 to 94 percent of crash events overall (Dingus et al., 2016). Lighting conditions form a second shared vulnerability: poor illumination weakens detection of delineation devices, reduces the lead time available for speed adjustment, and is associated with higher fatal outcomes in severe work zone crashes regardless of crash type (Li and Bai, 2009; Zhang and Hassan, 2019). Lane closures and the resulting narrowing of available travel lanes also operate across categories, as forced merging reduces lateral clearance and produces queuing conditions that underlie both rear end events and the inattentive path deviations that result in intrusions (Yang et al., 2015; Nasrollahzadeh et al., 2021). Work zone length increases cumulative driver exposure for both event types. Rural location operates as a shared severity amplifier through higher prevailing approach speeds, increasing fatality probability irrespective of crash type (Li and Bai, 2009; Zhang and Hassan, 2019).

### 2.5 Distinguishing Factors Between Crash Types

Several variables operate distinctly across the two categories, reflecting the difference between path deviation and in lane speed management failure. Delineation and channelization quality and the performance of intrusion alert systems are central to intrusion outcomes, since they determine whether the driver registers the workspace boundary in time and whether workers receive enough warning to clear the impact zone (NASEM, 2023a; Thapa and Mishra, 2021). Physical barrier deployment, including portable concrete barriers, is exclusively relevant to intrusion mitigation and has no direct bearing on rear end frequency, although Bryden et al. (2000) noted that barrier impacts themselves can be severe events given the rigid deflection characteristics. Queue management and the effectiveness of advance warning sign sequences, dynamic message signs, end of queue warning systems, and consistency between posted and operating speeds are predominantly non-intrusion factors that govern rear end probability (Yang et al., 2015; U.S. Department of Transportation, 2025). Commercial vehicle involvement elevates risk in both categories, but through different pathways: in intrusion events, the mass and approach speed of the intruding truck dominate the energy delivered to a worker or object struck (Bryden et al., 2000), while in rear end events truck involvement concentrates crash energy in the smaller struck vehicle (Islam, 2022). Speed exceedance also operates somewhat differently across the two categories, with higher approach speeds increasing the likelihood and severity of intrusion events through reduced path correction time, while in non-intrusion events the speed differential between approach and queue speeds is the more direct severity driver (Zhang and Hassan, 2019; U.S. Department of Transportation, 2025).

### 2.6 Research Gap

The literature reviewed above demonstrates that several contributing factors appear in both bodies of work but are not equally well covered. Driver inattention, speeding, lane closures, work zone length, lighting conditions, rural location, and large truck involvement are reported in non-intrusion studies (Khattak et al., 2002; Li and Bai, 2009; Ghasemzadeh and Ahmed, 2019; Mokhtarimousavi et al., 2019; Nasrollahzadeh et al., 2021; Islam, 2022) and also appear in the intrusion focused studies (Bryden et al., 2000; Ullman et al., 2008; Ullman et al., 2010; Ullman et al., 2011; Thapa and Mishra, 2021; Das et al., 2023). Even so, the intrusion oriented studies cover these factors with a narrower lens, often through descriptive characterization, single state data, or experiments built around specific alert systems, and they do not span the full range of behavioral, environmental, geometric, and configuration variables that the non-intrusion literature has examined.

In addition, several variables that appear prominently in the intrusion literature have not received comparable treatment in the non-intrusion literature. These include Work Zone Intrusion Alert System layout and accuracy, sensor to worker distance, deliberate noncompliance behavior leading to intentional intrusion, and the role of channelizing device and short term setup quality (Bryden et al., 2000; Ullman et al., 2011; Thapa and Mishra, 2021). Because the existing intrusion specific work is not comprehensive, additional factors may exist whose effects on intrusion outcomes have not yet been measured, the magnitude of known effects has not been fully estimated, and most studies rest on limited datasets that cover only one state or one experimental configuration. Many earlier studies have also targeted either the prediction of severity or the prediction of occurrence in isolation. The present paper aims to bring these threads together by examining intrusion and non-intrusion crashes within a common analytical framework, comparing contributing factor sets directly, and assessing both occurrence and severity outcomes.

## 3. METHODOLOGY

A comparative review of the methodological choices used in the work zone safety literature provides the basis for the analytical strategy adopted in this paper. Table 2 summarizes the principal studies cited in this review with respect to the method applied, the factors considered, and the limitations associated with the methodology, as reported in the source studies or as evident from their data and design.

| Thapa and Mishra (2021) | Survival analysis of worker reaction in emulated intrusion experiments evaluating three Work Zone Intrusion Alert Systems (Intellicone, AWARE, Worker Alert System) | Speed of the intruding vehicle, sensor-to-worker distance, system detection and alert accuracy | Based on emulated intrusions rather than real crash data; limited number of trials; results tied to the specific systems and configurations tested |
| Das, Tabesh, Dadashova, and Dobrovolny (2023) | Cluster correspondence analysis of TxDOT fatal and injury encroachment crashes, 2016-2019 | Driver inattention, roadway divided/undivided configuration, median presence, curvature, traffic control conditions | Single state dataset; clustering is descriptive and does not estimate marginal effects or predict severity; sensitive to category encoding choices |
